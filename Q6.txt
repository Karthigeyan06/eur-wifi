6. Differences Between 802.11a and 802.11b
802.11a:

Released in 1999, operates on the 5 GHz frequency band
Provides a maximum data rate of 54 Mbps
Uses OFDM (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing) as its modulation technique
Offers up to 23 non-overlapping channels, which significantly reduces interference between nearby networks
Indoor range is approximately 35 meters
Less interference because the 5 GHz band was less crowded at the time
Weaker wall and obstacle penetration due to the higher frequency
Was more expensive to manufacture initially

802.11b:

Also released in 1999, operates on the 2.4 GHz frequency band
Provides a maximum data rate of only 11 Mbps
Uses DSSS (Direct Sequence Spread Spectrum) as its modulation technique
Offers only 3 non-overlapping channels, leading to more network congestion in dense areas
Indoor range is approximately 38 meters, slightly better than 802.11a
More prone to interference from Bluetooth devices, microwave ovens, and neighboring networks
Better wall penetration due to the lower frequency
Was cheaper to produce and became widely adopted in homes and offices
