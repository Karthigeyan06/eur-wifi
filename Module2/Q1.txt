1. Brief about SplitMAC Architecture and how it improves the AP's performance
SplitMAC architecture is a design where the MAC (Medium Access Control) layer functions are divided between the Access Point (AP) and the Wireless LAN Controller (WLC). 
Instead of the AP handling all wireless functions on its own, responsibilities are split so that time-sensitive, real-time tasks stay at the AP while management and policy-related tasks are handled centrally at the WLC.
The AP handles real-time functions such as beaconing, sending probe responses, acknowledging packets, encrypting and decrypting frames, and managing frame queuing. 
The WLC takes care of non-real-time functions like client authentication, association handling, key management, QoS policy enforcement, and roaming decisions.
This improves performance because the AP is no longer burdened with heavy processing tasks. It can dedicate its resources entirely to radio-level operations, which are time-critical.
The WLC, being centralized, can enforce consistent policies across all APs, make smarter roaming decisions, and perform RF optimization like adjusting transmit power and channels dynamically. 
APs also become simpler and cheaper since they don't need powerful hardware to handle complex logic.
